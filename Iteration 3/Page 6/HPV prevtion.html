How to Prevent Human Papillomavirus (HPV)


Overview

Although it looks unavoidable that people get HPV infection, there are two methods to lower the risk of getting of getting or spreading HPV:

  • Positive prevetion: get the HPV vaccine.
  • Negative prevetion: use condoms or dental dams when your have sexual activity.

This page will introduce more on prosition prevation, which is vaccine.



Who should get vaccine

People who have had sexual activity may be infected by some types of HPV. Thus, we recommend you to be immunised before you become sexually active. In Australia, school students aged between 12-13 can start to take HPV vaccine. Children over 9-year-old can also take vaccine if their immunise system is weak.


Who shouldn't get vaccine

Just be aware that not all people are suitable to take vaccine. Those people who have serious allergy or previous allergy should be careful to take HPV vaccine. Pregnant women and people having bleeding disorder should ask doctor before take any vaccine.



HPV vaccine in Australia

In Australia, the Commonwealth and state and territory governments set up the National Immunisation Program (NIP) in order to reduce the number of disease people. Vaccines are free for people who are eligible under the NIP.

The NIP offers offers two types of HPV vaccines:

  • 2vHPV (Cervarix): that immunise HPV types 16 and 18.
  • 9vHPV (Gardasil®9): that immunise HPV types 6, 11, 16, 18, 31, 33, 45, 52 and 58.
